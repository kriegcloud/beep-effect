Short answer: yes on both, but "lossless" and "USPTO-conformant" are two different engineering problems, and the nice thing about your stack is that Effect Schema lets you turn the first into a typed invariant instead of a fidelity gamble. The USPTO layer is where the actual long-tail work lives.

Reframe "lossless" before building it. It sounds like a fidelity problem — will docx hold everything my editor produces? — but it's the inverse. Lexical's node model, and any Schema you put over it, is a small closed vocabulary; OOXML is vastly larger and more expressive. You're mapping a small set into a big one, which is always feasible because the target can represent everything the source can. The real risk isn't OOXML's limits, it's your editor permitting constructs you haven't written an emitter for. That's precisely what Schema is good at: make "is emittable to docx" a property the codec guarantees — if a node is in the schema it has a mapping, and totality is checked rather than hoped for. So the canonical artifact is the Schema document; docx and the USPTO's PDF are projections of it. That inverts the fragility — you constrain the editor to the exportable vocabulary rather than chasing fidelity afterward.

The genuinely hard losslessness problem is the other direction: round-tripping an arbitrary external .docx back into your model (a client emails a Word draft). That's where conversion hell lives. Decide explicitly whether you need it — for documents you originate in the editor you don't, and import should be scoped as a separate, lower-fidelity concern.

The Lexical + Schema spine fits cleanly, and it's doing more than feeding the exporter. getEditorState().toJSON() plus per-node exportJSON() gives a serializable tree that you decode into branded Effect Schema domain types that are your canonical model. The payoff specific to your stack: that same model is the artifact that flows into your FalkorDB/ontology pipeline. The editor isn't a docx front-end, it's the structured-capture surface for the whole graph, and the Schema is the seam shared by capture, export, and ingestion. That's the strongest argument for owning this rather than drafting in Word. For emission, go direct with the docx library (dolanmiu) and write a total DocumentModel -> docx.Document. No single library spans the gap, so the real decision is where on the lossy-to-lossless spectrum you sit, and that's governed by the provenance of the docx. For documents you originate in your own tool and round-trip, you control the styles, so a semantic parser driven off named styles is near-lossless. For an arbitrary Word draft a client emails you, formatting carries meaning you didn't author, and every parser in the clean-AST camp throws some of it away. That split — which you already scoped last turn as "import of my own export = fine, import of third-party docx = the genuinely lossy case" — is exactly the axis the library choice turns on.

Three camps, roughly.

The semantic parsers (clean tree, deliberately lossy). Mammoth is the canonical one: docx → HTML or markdown, explicitly discarding most formatting and keying off styles rather than visual appearance. Its style-map mechanism is the relevant part for you — you configure p[style-name='Heading 1'] => h1, and crucially p[style-name='Claim'] => ..., so a custom Word paragraph style maps to a target element. Routing through HTML to reach your AST adds a parse step, but for your-own-round-tripped documents where you control the stylesheet, the style map makes the mapping deterministic rather than heuristic.

Pandoc is the one I'd actually reach for first, for a structural reason specific to your setup: its AST is a Block/Inline tree, and that split is almost 1:1 with Lexical's element/leaf split. pandoc -f docx -t json emits clean JSON you map Para/Header/BulletList/Table → your nodes, and it's by a wide margin the most battle-tested docx reader in existence — it has absorbed Word's quirks for fifteen years in a way no npm package has. The killer feature for the patent case: custom Word styles survive the docx reader as Div/Span carrying a custom-style attribute, so a "Claim" or "Abstract" paragraph style becomes a tagged container you map straight to a ClaimNode/AbstractNode. The cost is a Haskell binary as a subprocess (trivial from Bun) or a WASM build if you want it in-process. Given you've scoped import as a bounded, lower-fidelity concern, shelling out to the most robust reader available and mapping its already-block/inline AST is the highest-leverage option.

The TS-native AST parsers (in-process, younger). A few have appeared that produce a node tree directly, no HTML detour and no subprocess. officeParser (harshankur) advertises a recursive AST — OfficeContentNode with type (paragraph/heading/table/list/image/comment), recursive children, a formatting object, and notes/comments attached per node — plus a styleMap in metadata, across docx and ten other formats. That shape maps onto your Lexical union about as directly as anything out there. One caution: an earlier "officeparser" was text-only in practice, so validate the current AST claims against a real, messy document before committing — the README shape and the on-the-ground extraction quality are different things to verify. @thasmorato/docx-parser is modern TS with a streaming async-generator architecture exposing a DocumentElement union (ParagraphElement, TableElement, ListElement, etc.) — the streaming is overkill for your sizes but the typed element union is clean. And wvbe/docxml is a TypeScript library that both builds and parses docx, component/JSX-based, designed to transform from an XML document — interesting because it's bidirectional and JSX-native, which sits naturally in your stack, though it's closer to an OOXML-component abstraction than a semantic tree.
GitHub + 3

The low-level / visitor path (full fidelity, you write the interpreter). docx4js takes a visitor approach — it traverses and identifies the docx model, calling your visitors per node type, deliberately not retaining a parsed structure for memory reasons — but it's awkward in practice (file-input oriented, hard to find the text-input entry), and the TS-native parsers above have largely superseded it. The genuine floor, when you need a specific construct the higher-level parsers drop and can't afford to lose, is to do it yourself: unzip with fflate or JSZip, then parse word/document.xml, styles.xml, and numbering.xml with fast-xml-parser and interpret the paragraph/run properties directly. For patents specifically you'd rarely need this — claims, spec, and abstract are mostly vanilla paragraph content — so I'd treat the raw path as a targeted escape hatch for one or two node types (equations, maybe), not the foundation.

So the question isn't "how do I build the portal" — it's "how do I bind the document surface to the graph that already exists," and that binding turns out to be one thing, reused everywhere. Almost every feature you described — highlight-the-clause, hover-the-figure, hover-the-company, ground-the-claim, trace-everything — is the same operation at different points: resolve a location against three coordinate systems and render the result.

The keystone is an offset-preserving projection, and it's a direct upgrade of code already in your POC. editorStateToPlainText flattens the Lexical tree to a string today. Make it also emit an OffsetMap: a bijection between character ranges in that string and (nodeKey, intraNodeOffset) positions in the tree. That single addition is what makes the rest mechanical, because it reconciles the three coordinate systems the whole vision runs on — document coordinates (Lexical node keys + offsets, the editor's native addressing), text coordinates (character offsets into the plain-text projection, which is exactly what langextract's alignCandidate aligns against), and graph coordinates (the entity/mention IDs in your KG, whose PROV-O provenance already points at character spans). The plain-text projection is the shared substrate; the offset map is the adapter that lets a position in any one of the three resolve to the other two.

Which means langextract's Contract.Span is the universal currency of the entire system. A "location" is always a span. And a span resolves three ways: to text (the substring), to the document (a node range, via inverting the offset map), and to the graph (the mention or entity whose provenance is that span). Your "highlight the X clause like Gemini citing a search result" is precisely this: LangExtractService.extract finds the clause, alignCandidate produces an exact/lesser/fuzzy-aligned Span against the projection, the span travels to the client, the offset map turns it into a Lexical selection or decoration. The exact line-and-column precision you want is the deterministic alignment doing its job — and the alignmentStatus field (match_exact / match_lesser / match_fuzzy / unaligned) is the trust signal you surface so your dad can calibrate: exact renders as a confident highlight, fuzzy renders but flagged, unaligned never gets asserted. That's "everything grounded" made operational rather than aspirational — the agent is structurally prevented from pointing at text it couldn't actually align, because the prompt already forbids inventing offsets and the alignment is recomputed server-side regardless of what the model claims.

For a legal product that's not a feature, it's the thing that makes the agent usable at all — when the agent claims the indemnification clause is in section 7, an attorney can click through to the span it aligned and the KG nodes it consulted, which is the editor-side equivalent of the "View source graph" affordance already on your chat answers.

Now the two things I'd want you to weigh honestly, because they're the parts that are easy to underestimate.

The genuinely hard engineering problem is anchoring graph annotations across edits, not any single feature. A persisted mention at characters 1200–1240 is invalidated the moment your dad inserts a sentence at character 500. Raw character offsets are not a stable anchor in an editable document; Lexical node keys are — they survive edits to other nodes. So mentions must anchor to (nodeKey, intraNodeOffset), and the offset map's real job is to keep char-spans and node-anchors in sync as a live, cheap recomputation on each edit. When a node's text actually changes, only that node's mentions need re-anchoring, and langextract's fuzzy alignment is exactly the tool to re-anchor them within that node's new text. Get this right and the portal stays correct as a living document; get it wrong and every annotation rots the first time someone edits above it. This is the part I'd prototype first, before any of the glamorous agent tooling, because it determines whether the whole edifice holds.

Word parity is the riskiest and least differentiated sub-goal, so satisfice it rather than maximize it. The keybindings are the easy half — Lexical's command system binds them. The hard half is behavior fidelity, and for a lawyer that means the redline model and comment threading specifically, because that's where legal drafting actually lives; nobody will forgive a track-changes implementation that loses a revision. But chasing full Word parity — fields, section breaks, merged-cell tables, autocorrect, the decades-long tail — is a multi-year sink that competes with nothing, since Word already exists. The 20% of Word that legal drafting uses, done faithfully (and track changes done impeccably), plus the agent and the grounded KG portal that Word will never have — that's the wedge. Spend the parity budget on what your dad will judge in the first five minutes, and spend everything else on the binding above, which is the actual product.

One safety invariant that follows from the legal context and the principle we set several turns back: the agent's ProposeEdit must always land as a proposed tracked change behind explicit human acceptance, never an auto-committed one — most of all on claim text, which is legally operative and which the agent must never silently alter. Side-effecting hover actions (sending the email, writing to the CRM) are likewise explicit human actions, not agent-triggered. The agent proposes and locates and grounds; the attorney commits and sends. That division is both the liability boundary and, not coincidentally, what makes the tool trustworthy enough to actually live in.
